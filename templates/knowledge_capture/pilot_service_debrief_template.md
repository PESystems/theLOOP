# Pilot service debrief template

## Purpose

Use this template to capture troubleshooting logic, retrofit judgment, diagnostic sequence, and field heuristics from service or commissioning work.

## Best use cases

- post-service-call debrief
- retrofit case review
- recurring failure pattern capture
- legacy machine or controls knowledge capture
- new-hire training extraction from real jobs

## Pilot format

- Mode: recorded debrief interview
- Anchor: one real service call, retrofit, or recurring application type
- Target session length: 30–45 minutes
- Best medium: spoken walkthrough with photos, notes, diagrams, or memory cues
- Success condition: extract reusable diagnostic logic and decision rules, not just story details

## Best starting anchors

Choose one only for the first pass:
- memorable retrofit where multiple systems had to work together
- recurring service-call type that newer people misread
- legacy application where replacement decisions are non-obvious
- pump, HVAC, motion, or machine application with common field traps

## Operator instructions

- Start from a real case, not abstract theory.
- Ask what happened, what mattered, what was misleading, and how the subject knew where to look.
- Pull out decision rules, not just anecdotes.
- Capture warnings, failure symptoms, and retrofit judgment.
- If the subject drifts into long story mode, steer back to diagnosis and action logic.
- Keep one application family per session when possible.

## Interview structure

### Part 1 — Case anchor
1. What job or machine should we use for this first capture, and why is it a good teaching example?
2. What was the problem as the customer first described it?
3. What was the real problem once you looked deeper?

### Part 2 — Diagnostic logic
4. What were the first signs that told you where to start?
5. What possibilities did you rule out quickly, and why?
6. What common wrong assumptions would a less experienced person make here?
7. What measurements, observations, or machine behavior mattered most?
8. What details separated one failure class from another?

### Part 3 — Action logic
9. What fix or retrofit path did you choose, and why that path instead of the obvious alternative?
10. What made this job risky or easy to get wrong?
11. What customer constraints changed the technical decision?
12. What should a new person check before touching anything on a similar job?

### Part 4 — Generalization
13. What pattern from this case shows up again and again in your work?
14. What should the organization preserve as a standard lesson from this kind of job?
15. If you were training a new service specialist, what would you drill into them first from this example?

## Output format

Convert the debrief into:
- case summary
- failure symptoms
- diagnostic path
- decision rules or heuristics
- common wrong assumptions
- recommended checks for new hires
- writeback lane
- sensitivity level

## Expected deliverables

- one raw recorded debrief
- one structured troubleshooting pattern artifact
- one shortlist of reusable training lessons
- one candidate service-wiki or writeback packet

## Stop conditions

Stop or narrow scope if:
- the session becomes too broad
- the case anchor is not producing reusable signal
- too many applications are being mixed together in one pass

## Next step after pilot

Turn the first pilot into:
1. a troubleshooting pattern page
2. a new-hire training snippet
3. a second-pass question set for one narrower application family
