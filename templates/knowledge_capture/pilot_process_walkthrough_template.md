# Pilot process walkthrough template

## Purpose

Use this template to capture operational process knowledge from people who hold critical business, purchasing, billing, CRM, or workflow knowledge that is poorly documented.

## Best use cases

- finance and billing workflow capture
- purchasing and vendor-update handling
- quote-system or CRM operating logic
- recurring back-office tasks with hidden dependencies
- handoff design for junior staff training

## Pilot format

- Mode: screen-share walkthrough, desk-side walkthrough, or narrated live process
- Anchor: one real recurring workflow
- Target session length: 20–45 minutes
- Best medium: live system walkthrough with the operator narrating decisions and exceptions
- Success condition: extract the real process, key decisions, exception handling, and failure points

## Best starting anchors

Choose one only for the first pass:
- billing or receivables flow
- purchasing and vendor price-update workflow
- quote intake and routing process
- CRM maintenance workflow
- recurring month-end or weekly operational routine

## Operator instructions

- Follow one real workflow from trigger to completion.
- Ask what happens, what can go wrong, what shortcuts are used, and what exceptions matter.
- Capture decision points, not just button clicks.
- Treat software screens as memory aids, not the whole process.
- Watch for hidden handoffs, dependency points, and external relationship knowledge.

## Interview structure

### Part 1 — Trigger and scope
1. What starts this workflow?
2. What tells you this process needs to happen now?
3. What systems, files, or people are involved?

### Part 2 — Standard path
4. What are the normal steps from start to finish?
5. Which steps are essential versus habitual?
6. What information do you need before you begin?
7. What outputs are produced by the end?

### Part 3 — Decision points and exceptions
8. Where do you make judgment calls instead of following a fixed rule?
9. What exceptions appear most often?
10. What can go wrong if someone follows the visible steps but misses the underlying logic?
11. Which contacts, vendors, or internal people matter at key points?

### Part 4 — Transferability
12. What part of this workflow would a junior person most likely misunderstand?
13. What would you standardize first if you were training someone new?
14. What should become a checklist, and what should remain judgment-based?
15. What one failure should future staff be warned about early?

## Output format

Convert the walkthrough into:
- workflow summary
- trigger
- required inputs
- standard path
- decision points
- exception handling
- dependencies and relationships
- recommended training or writeback lane
- sensitivity level

## Expected deliverables

- one raw walkthrough capture
- one structured process map summary
- one exception-and-risk list
- one candidate onboarding or writeback packet

## Stop conditions

Stop or narrow scope if:
- multiple workflows are being mixed together
- the walkthrough becomes pure click-tracking without decision logic
- sensitive financial or personnel material needs separate handling

## Next step after pilot

Turn the first pilot into:
1. a process summary artifact
2. a training checklist draft
3. a follow-up question set focused on exceptions and edge cases
