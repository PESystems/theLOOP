# Pilot micro capture template

## Purpose

Use this template for low-friction knowledge capture when the subject has limited time, limited energy, or the goal is to extract a small number of high-value knowledge objects quickly.

## Best use cases

- senior expert with limited availability
- fragile or time-constrained sessions
- first-pass capture before deeper interviews
- short asynchronous response capture
- voice-note or email-response follow-up

## Capture format

- Mode: short questionnaire, short call, or voice note
- Target session length: 10–20 minutes
- Success condition: extract 3–7 durable knowledge objects without overloading the subject

## Capture priorities

Replace these with the domain-specific priorities for the session:
1. key decisions people make too fast to explain
2. risks newer staff tend to miss
3. shortcuts, heuristics, or fit judgments not obvious from documentation
4. customer, vendor, or application knowledge likely to disappear if not captured now

## Operator instructions

- Keep the tone calm and low-pressure.
- Prefer short-answer prompts.
- Do not chase biography unless it directly explains useful operating knowledge.
- Stop early if the session starts to feel burdensome.
- Preserve partial signal rather than forcing completion.
- If one answer opens a high-value thread, follow it and drop lower-priority questions.

## Prompt set

### Section A — Fast extraction
1. What are 3 things newer people tend to underestimate in this area?
2. What are 3 decisions you make quickly that a less experienced person would likely get wrong?
3. What situations require extra caution before quoting, diagnosing, or acting?
4. What recurring mistake shows up early and causes trouble later?
5. What products, vendors, or application types require special fit judgment not obvious from documentation?

### Section B — High-value shortcuts
6. When a request sounds simple but is actually risky, what are your first checks?
7. What are your fastest rules for deciding whether to proceed, investigate first, or decline?
8. What details should a new hire always gather before escalating for help?
9. What warning signs usually mean there is hidden complexity behind the request?
10. If you could leave one page of advice for a future person in this role, what must be on it?

### Section C — Optional follow-up prompts
11. Are there specific vendors, contacts, or customers worth preserving in the knowledge base?
12. Are there recurring patterns worth turning into a checklist?
13. What one thing is most worth preserving now before it is forgotten?

## Output format

Convert each useful response into:
- Claim or heuristic
- Context
- Confidence: verified / heuristic / needs follow-up
- Follow-up needed: yes / no
- Writeback lane
- Sensitivity: normal / review / restricted

## Expected deliverables

- one short raw capture
- one structured extraction summary
- one follow-up queue
- one candidate writeback packet

## Stop conditions

Stop immediately if:
- the subject appears tired or uncomfortable
- the answers become repetitive from fatigue
- the session starts to feel like work burden instead of assisted capture

## Next step after pilot

Turn captured output into:
1. a structured extraction artifact
2. a shortlist of follow-up questions for a later micro-pass
3. candidate writeback targets by topic or lane
